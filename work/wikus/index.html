WIKUS

INTRO

As a derivative of the C-bot, this is a 3D printer that I have been working on for the past 2 years. The design is based on a CoreXY method of motion, which is fairly uncommon for desktop 3D printers.

I quickly learned, after assembling my first 3D printer kit, that the actual process of building printers is where I find the greatest utility. So in 2014 I set out to make one of the most capable, unique FDM desktop 3D printers. Now, after 2 years of development on and off, WIKUS was born. The design has taken great inspiration from Carl Feniak's C-Bot build, but with many modifications to satisfy the following design tenets:

- Clean. A professional look with little exposed wiring.

- Quiet. No unnecessary noise created from linear motion and cooling.

- Fast. Minimize gantry mass for quick movements.

- Capable. Massive build volume, dual extrusion. Easy to upgrade.

Linear Motion

The method of motion on this printer is definitely one of its strong suits. The V-slot extrusions, paired with delrin V-wheels, proves to be, for all intents and purposes, silent.

As for speed and accuracy, the light gantry (custom cut 1/8'' aluminium on university water jet) makes travel speeds in excess of 100 mm/s not uncommon. Prints with 100 micron layer height have been achieved with great success (vase in top picture).

A CoreXY technique has been employed. I find it very nice just in the fact that there is no translational movement of any stepper motor. My rationale with choosing it was, with a bowden extruder setup, to greatly minimize the inertia of the system.

Part Sourcing

I was able to canabalize the stepper motors and other basic electronics from my previous printer, and I felt it beneficial to source the remaining parts from only a few big vendors to reduce shipping costs. I sourced the V-slot extrusions and components from Openbuilds, and the remaining extrusions from Misumi. Newark was the main source of fasteners. I've compiled a complete bill of materials for this build, complete with suppliers and prices.

Auto Bed Leveling

The first iterations of WIKUS involved a simple set of leveling screws to level the entire bed. However, when the bed is 20cm x 40cm and not perfectly flat, this is an impossible task. To open up use of the entire bed for larger prints, auto leveling was a necessity. Instead of the generic inductive sensors, I went for a small servo controlled sensor called the BLTouch. I use it to probe 16 points (10 micron accuracy) on the bed and performing a least squares interpolation to live adjust the height during printing.

What's Next?

The journey of WIKUS continues, as it is easily modified as discussed above. The current aim is to use the printer as a platform for experimental development of automation technologies that I feel are lacking in the consumer printing field. With Octoprint and auto-bed leveling now installed, a means to remove parts from the build platform autonomously is one of the final steps in truly operating the printer remotely from anywhere. Furthermore, a way to switch filaments remotely, similarly to Josef Prusa's Multimaterial Upgrade , are currently in the works.

Stay tuned.
